Tableau Data Analysis of Hollywood's Most Profitable Stories

Data Selection

We found this dataset called "Hollywood's Most Profitable Stories" on Tableau Public's Sample Data page. We were interested in conducting data analysis related to films and their total profitability, looking at factors such as audience score, worldwide gross revenue, genre, and the studio that released the film.

We were looking for a dataset that had many categorical variables to allow for sufficient data analysis in Tableau, as we had encountered other datasets that were not as suitable for this approach. We had tried previously to use a dataset with university data from the United States, but it had many percentages that were not linked to other variables in the dataset in any way - making data analysis much more difficult.

We took into account that this dataset was listed on Tableau's website, as we had looked at a dataset from Kaggle, where the volume of available datasets is much larger, but they can be uploaded by anyone in the Kaggle community. We previously tried to use a dataset that was reportedly from WHO, but actually contained very inaccurate data after completing data validation with facts about various countries.

Data Processing

We were fortunate that we didn't have to do much data processing with this dataset. The dataset is relatively small, with only 75 movies included. For one of the entries, the lead studio category variable was null, so we researched the movie and added an entry for that variable. There are a couple of data entries without a profitability value, but we did not have the full range of data in order to compute that ourselves.

Exploration

We separated the data exploration process in Tableau by dividing it into four different sections: looking at films by genre, looking at films over time, time series data, and looking at films with audience ratings. We were trying to use the categorical data and go as in depth as possible, so separating our analysis into these sections helped to focus our story and analysis.



Movies are expensive to make, so it’s not surprising that when we analyzed highest-grossing movies and the most profitable ones, we learned that a movie that was high-grossing was not necessarily as profitable as we would think. Here we see that the top most profitable movie Fireproof with a profit of $66.93 million was not even in the top 50% of highest-grossing movies, suggesting that although the revenue it generated was not necessarily very high compared to other movies. It’s likely that it had a low cost of production, making the profit margin very large even when it didn’t generate a high revenue. Generally, however, there seems to be a positive correlation between gross revenue and profitability. For example, The Twilight Saga: New Moon movie is the third most profitable movie as well as the highest-grossing movie in the dataset. This relationship is especially evident in the third visualization, where we see that the higher-grossing movies have higher profitability.




We focused on looking at the films by studio and genre. First, we created two bubble charts to show the number of films each studio produced that were used in this dataset, compared to the cumulative worldwide gross revenue that each studio received as a result of these films. This answers the question of how prevalent each studio is in the dataset, compared to the gross revenue they received.

Moving on to looking at genre, we looked at the count of each genre and the types of genres featured in this dataset. We then created a bstacked ar chart that depicts how many films of each genre that each studio produced. It answers the question of what genres are most frequently produced in a dataset of profitable stories. This chart could also be compared to the worldwide gross revenue per studio bubble chart, looking at which film genres are produced/profitable by lead studios of the highest worldwide gross revenue.

Finally, we compared the cumulative profitability and the worldwide gross revenue per genre in a side-by-side bar chart. This combines the previous bar chart that looked at genres by studios and the initial bubble chart of worldwide gross revenue by studio. It removes the studio variable, but better highlights the most profitable genres, as well as comparing their worldwide gross revenue to the end result of profitability.



Before analyzing the dataset, we thought that there would be a positive correlation between movie rating and the financial performance of a film. There is indeed a positive correlation when we look at Rotten Tomatoes scores and the profitability and gross revenue generated; however, it is not as strong as we initially thought it would be. It also seems that the range of profitability for films is not as wide as the range of worldwide gross for the dataset, so it appears that profitability doesn’t vary as significantly from movie to movie as it does for worldwide gross. When we look at the actual audience rating and its relationship to the financial performance of a movie, we see that the correlation is stronger, especially when analyzing the worldwide gross. This makes sense as movies that tend to do well with a wider general audience will generate more hype and higher ticket sales than a movie that appeals more to a smaller circle of professional critics, even if it has a lower Rotten Tomatoes score. However, generally we see that audience ratings and Rotten Tomatoes scores tend to have a positive correlation.



We also decided to look at the data over time and see how different variables have shifted over the years. First, we looked at how many movies were made each year in our dataset. We noticed an almost cyclical nature, with approximately 19 movies in 2008 and 2010 and between 11 and 13 movies in 2007, 2009 and 2011. Although our dataset is limited, we felt these films are comparable to each other and thus our analysis can still be used to draw conclusions about trends in the data over time. We then examined each studio to see who was increasing the number of films on the list from 2007 to 2011 and found that independent films were becoming more popular since they had the greatest increase in the "Hollywood's Most Profitable Movies" dataset. We also looked at the number of films by genre and saw a sharp decrease in comedy movies in 2011. Next, we constructed streamgraphs for gross revenue by studio per year and found that the comedy genre and Disney stuidos collected the most revenue during the majority of years from 2007 to 2011. In contrast, independent studios were the most profitable in 2007, 2008 and 2011 while dramas were the most profitable in 2008 and comedy was the most profitable every other year. Finally, another trend we looked at over time was the rotten tomato score and saw that Sony had a sharp increase in its average rotten tomato score in 2011, due to the high score that "Midnight in Paris" received. Interestingly, animation had the highest average rotten tomato score out of any genre up until 2011, when comedy passed it because of the low score that "Gnomeo and Juliet" received. Who would've thought Shakespeare would be the one to bring the rating down.

Final Explanation

From these analyses, we came away with some key conclusions about profitable stories in Hollywood. The most prevalent genre of these profitable stories was Comedy, which was the largest genre by count of films, worldwide gross revenue, and profitability. The most films were made by independent studios, followed by Warner Brothers. Disney and Warner Brothers made the most worldwide gross revenue and had mostly comedy films by count, but independent studios made more romance films. We also learned that while there was a generally positive correlation between high-grossing movies and profitable films, the correlation was not particularly strong and a high-grossing movie is not necessarily a profitable one, due to high production costs. We also learned that when it came to profitability and worldwide gross, audience opinion matters more than professional critics, as there is a stronger correlation between financial performance and overall audience opinion. We found that over time the sharpest decrease was seen in the number of comedy movies in 2011, while romance films were on the rise. We were surprised to learn that animated films had the highest rotten tomato score from 2008 to 2010 and noticed that comedy and drama films seem to be getting higher rotten tomato scores as of recently. We enjoyed being able to analyze this dataset and are excited to see if these trends among Hollywood's most profitable stories continue.
